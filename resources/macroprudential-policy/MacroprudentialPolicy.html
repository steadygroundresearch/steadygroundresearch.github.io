Macroprudential policy is the regulatory approach designed to safeguard the stability of the financial system as a whole — a perspective that emerged largely in response to the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, when individually well-capitalised banks still failed to prevent a system-wide collapse.

Connection to Financial Stability Macroprudential policy exists to safeguard financial stability by containing systemic risk. See the Financial Stability section for how that risk is defined and identified.
💡

Did You Know? The term "macroprudential" existed in obscure BIS discussions since the late 1970s — but the 2008 crisis turned it into the backbone of modern financial regulation, leading directly to new bodies like the European Systemic Risk Board and the U.S. Financial Stability Oversight Council, both established in 2010.

Tier 1 Foundations Start here

What is Macroprudential Policy?

A policy approach aimed at safeguarding the stability of the financial system as a whole, rather than the safety of any single institution.

Macro- vs. Microprudential

Microprudential policy focuses on the soundness of individual institutions. Macroprudential policy focuses on risk to the system as a whole — the two are complementary, not competing.

Tier 2 Building Blocks Operationalising the objective

Systemic Risk as the Stated Objective

Macroprudential policy is explicitly built around containing systemic risk — the risk that a shock cascades through the financial system rather than staying contained. See the Financial Stability section for how this risk (and related concepts like contagion and interconnectedness) is defined and diagnosed.

The Policy Cycle

In practice, macroprudential policy follows a repeating cycle: identify a build-up of risk, select an appropriate instrument, calibrate its intensity, then monitor and adjust as conditions evolve.
