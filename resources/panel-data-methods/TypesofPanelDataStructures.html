Panel data tracks units (i) across time (t) - but "panel" is not one shape. How units are sampled, how complete the grid is, and what scale the units sit at, all change which structure you are working with. Eleven structures, drawn from Baltagi, Wooldridge, Hsiao, and Arellano are outlined below.

Note:

The structures are not mutually exclusive - a real dataset is always a combination of several at once. A researcher rarely says just "panel data"; they say things like a "Short, Unbalanced, True Panel" or a "Long, Balanced, Macro Panel" - stacking one label from Time Depth, one from Completeness, one from Sample Design, and so on.

Showing all 11 structures

Completeness

Balanced Panel

no gaps — full grid

Every unit is observed in every time period — a complete N×T rectangle.

Explain
There are no gaps: each of the N units has a full run of T observations. This is the simplest case and the one most estimation theory is initially built around, since every unit contributes equally to the sample at every point in time.
Completeness

Unbalanced Panel

some cells missing

Some units are missing observations in some periods.

Explain
Gaps appear due to survey non-response, firms entering or exiting a market, deaths, migration, or discontinued data collection. This is the norm rather than the exception in real-world data — which is why it's discussed so heavily in the literature.
Time Depth

Short Panel

many units, few periods

Large number of units (N), but only a few time periods (T).

Explain
Typically single digits to low double-digits of time periods. Most household and firm surveys fall here — e.g., thousands of firms tracked for five years. The literature treats this as the "default" panel case.
Time Depth

Long Panel

few units, many periods

Small-to-moderate number of units (N), but many time periods (T).

Explain
Often spans decades of data. Country-level macro series — GDP, trade, inflation across countries — are the classic example of a long panel.
Aggregation Level

Micro Panel

many individual units

The unit of observation is an individual, household, or firm.

Explain
These panels usually have large N and dominate labor economics, household finance, and industrial organization research — think PSID or NLSY.
Aggregation Level

Macro Panel

few, larger units

The unit of observation is an aggregate — country, state, region, or industry.

Explain
These panels tend to have smaller N and are common in growth economics, international finance, and macro-development research — think Penn World Tables.
Sample Design

True Panel

same units, tracked

The exact same units are re-observed at each time point.

Explain
Same households, same firms — individually identifiable across every wave. This is what "panel data" means in the strictest sense.
Sample Design

Pseudo-Panel

different sample each column

A fresh random sample is drawn from the population each period.

Explain
Since individuals can't be tracked, researchers group observations into cohorts — e.g. birth year — and follow cohort averages over time as a stand-in for a true panel. This approach traces back to Deaton (1985) and is used when repeated cross-sectional surveys exist but a genuine panel doesn't.
Sample Design

Rotating Panel

units shift in/out over time

Units cycle in and out of the sample on a fixed schedule.

Explain
A household might stay in the sample for four consecutive quarters, then be replaced by a new household. Common in official labor force surveys — e.g. the US Current Population Survey — to reduce respondent burden while keeping some panel structure intact.
Dimensionality

Two-Dimensional Panel

unit × time only

The standard structure — just two indices, unit (i) and time (t).

Explain
This is what's implicitly assumed whenever "panel data" is mentioned without qualification — a single unit index crossed with a single time index.
Dimensionality

Three-Dimensional / Nested Panel

extra layer beyond unit × time

An extra structural dimension sits alongside unit and time.

Explain
Workers nested within firms nested within industries, observed over years; or students nested within classrooms within schools over time. Sometimes labeled "hierarchical" or "multilevel" panel data in the literature.
