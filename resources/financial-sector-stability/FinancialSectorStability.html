Financial stability describes a state in which the financial system — banks, markets, and infrastructure together — can absorb shocks and continue to allocate resources efficiently, without major disruption to the real economy.

💡

Did You Know? At the time of its 2008 collapse, Lehman Brothers held over $600 billion in assets — still the largest corporate bankruptcy in U.S. history, and a textbook case of how contagion spreads through a highly interconnected financial system.

What is Financial Stability?

A condition in which the financial system is resilient to shocks and continues to perform its core functions — channelling savings to productive investment, pricing risk, and enabling payments — without disruptive breakdown.

Tier 2 Building Blocks What threatens it

Systemic Risk

The risk that a shock to one part of the financial system triggers a broader breakdown, rather than staying contained to a single institution or market.

Contagion

The process by which distress spreads from one institution or market to others — through direct exposures, shared funding sources, or panic-driven behaviour.

Procyclicality

The tendency of financial behaviour to amplify the economic cycle — excessive risk-taking during booms, excessive caution and deleveraging during downturns.

Interconnectedness

The web of financial linkages between institutions and markets that allows shocks to transmit — a key channel through which contagion and systemic risk spread.

Tier 3 Applications — Assessing Stability Putting it into practice

Financial Stability Indicators

Quantitative measures — such as capital ratios, leverage, and credit growth — used to monitor the build-up of vulnerabilities across the financial system.

Stress Testing

Simulating the impact of adverse scenarios (e.g. a sharp recession or asset price collapse) on institutions or the system as a whole, to assess resilience before a real shock occurs.
